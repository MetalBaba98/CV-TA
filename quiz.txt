Q1. Which of the following correctly describe the 'recipe' for the scan/homography application?  [May 13 – Slide 7]
A.  Discover a 3×3 matrix H
B.  Discover a 4×4 matrix H
C.  Upgrade pixel p′ to homogeneous form u′₃₁
D.  Set (x,y) = (u₁/u₂, u₃/u₂)
E.  Set Image[x,y] = Image[x′,y′]
Answer: A, C, E
Explanation: The recipe: find a 3×3 H, upgrade p′ to u′₃₁, compute u = Hu′, extract (x,y) = (u₁/u₃, u₂/u₃), then copy pixel values.
Q2. To find H, the homography matrix, which steps are correct?  [May 13 – Slide 12]
A.  H appears as a solution to Ah = 0
B.  H appears as a solution to Ah = 1
C.  We perform SVD of A = UDVᵀ
D.  We focus on U; its last columns span the null space
E.  A will have a null space of dimension 1
Answer: A, C, E
Explanation: H solves Ah=0. SVD of A = UDVᵀ is used; we focus on V (not U), and the null space of A has dimension 1.
Q3. Which of the following are valid definitions or goals of computer vision according to the course?  [May 9 – Slides 4–6]

B.  Recovery of 3D geometry from images

D.  Replacing all deep learning with classical methods

Q4. Which statements about the pinhole camera model are correct?  [May 13 – Slides 21–22]
A.  A camera with a lens simulates the mathematical pinhole camera
B.  A barrier with a pinhole blocks most light rays
C.  The pinhole model always produces an inverted image in the physical setup
D.  The mathematical model uses a centre of projection and focal length f

Answer: A, B, D
Explanation: Lens cameras simulate the pinhole; the barrier blocks most rays; the mathematical model uses COP and focal length f. The non-inverted image model places the image plane in front.
Q5. For homogeneous coordinates in the context of image formation, which are true?  [May 13 – Slides 25–26]
A.  The relation between (x,y) and (X,Y,Z) can be linearised using homogeneous coordinates

C.  x = P₃₄ X_cam holds after dehomogenisation
D.  Homogeneous coordinates add an extra coordinate to represent projective points
E.  The 3D point (X,Y,Z) maps to image point (fX/Z, fY/Z)
Answer: A, C, D, E
Explanation: The tilde notation (~) denotes equality up to scale, not a real equality. All other statements are correct per the slides.
Q6. Which properties are preserved (invariant) under a projective transformation?  [May 16 – Slides 17–23; May 20 – Slides 17–23]
A.  Collinearity of points
B.  Parallelism of lines
C.  Cross ratio
D.  Angles between lines
E.  Incidence (a point lying on a line)
Answer: A, C, E
Explanation: Projectivity preserves collinearity and incidence. Parallelism is preserved only under affine transforms. Angles under similarities. Cross ratio is the key projective invariant.
Q7. Regarding the hierarchy of 2D transformations, which statements are correct?  [May 20 – Slides 20–23]
A.  A similarity transform preserves circles and squares
B.  An affine transform keeps parallel lines parallel
C.  A projective transform keeps parallel lines parallel
D.  The affine matrix has last row [0,0,1]
E.  A projective matrix has 8 degrees of freedom
Answer: A, B, D, E
Explanation: Projective transforms do NOT preserve parallelism — that is an affine invariant. All other statements match the slides.
Q8. In projective geometry (P²), which of the following are correct?  [May 16 – Slides 31–34; May 20 – Slides 7–10]
A.  A point is represented as an equivalence class of non-zero triplets
B.  (0,0,0)ᵀ is a valid projective point

D.  The triplet [kx, ky, kz] represents the same point as [x,y,z]
E.  A Cartesian point (x,y) corresponds to projective point [x,y,1]ᵀ
Answer: A, C, D, E
Explanation: (0,0,0)ᵀ is explicitly not allowed. All other statements are correct.
Q9. Which of the following are true about parallel lines in P²?  [May 16 – Slides 37–38; May 20 – Slides 13–14]
A.  They intersect at ideal points (last coordinate = 0)
B.  Two ideal points define an ideal line
C.  Their intersection is the cross product of their line triplets
D.  They have no intersection in P²
E.  The line at infinity is composed of all ideal points
Answer: A, B, C, E
Explanation: In P², parallel lines do intersect — at ideal points. Their intersection is the cross product of their triplets. Two ideal points form the ideal/line at infinity.
Q10. The cross ratio in P¹ has which of the following properties?  [May 20 – Slides 29–30]
A.  It is defined for four points
B.  It is invariant under projective transformations
C.  Its value changes if one point is ideal
D.  It does not depend on the homogeneous scale factor
E.  It is the key invariant of projective transforms in P²
Answer: A, B, D, E
Explanation: The cross ratio is defined for 4 points, is projectively invariant, scale-independent, and remains valid even when one point is ideal.

Q12. Which are practical notes the slides give about computing homographies from point correspondences?  [May 16 – Slide 21]

Q13. Which properties hold for the similarity transformation matrix?  [May 20 – Slide 21]

Q14. Which statements about vanishing points are correct?  [May 16 – Slides 28–29; May 20 – Slide 5]

Q15. In the 3D coordinate transformation between two camera frames, which are correct?  [May 16 – Slide 15]

Q16. Which are correct statements about the scope and relationships of computer vision?  [May 9 – Slides 7–9]

Q17. Regarding the relationship between two views of a plane, which are true?  [May 13 – Slides 3–5; May 16 – Slides 16–20]

Q18. Which of the following are true about a projectivity (homography) from P² to P²?  [May 20 – Slides 17–19]

E.  Any transform that preserves lines is a projectivity

Explanation: Projectivities do not generally map circles to circles — that is a property of similarity transforms. All other statements are correct.
Q19. In P¹ (the projective line), which are correct?  [May 20 – Slides 26–28]
A.  A point is a pair [x₁, x₂]ᵀ

E.  The matrix H in P¹ is homogeneous, so only 3 DOF are independent

Explanation: A 2×2 homogeneous matrix has 4 entries but only 3 independent DOF (known up to scale).
